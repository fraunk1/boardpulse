●boardpulse
Project overview · July 2026

One place to read what state medical boards are discussing

State medical and osteopathic boards publish their meeting minutes as PDFs scattered across dozens of separate websites — some clean text, some scanned paper. boardpulse is a working prototype that gathers those public documents into one library, summarizes every meeting with AI, and makes a year of national board activity searchable from a single dashboard.

Who

Built by Frank Meyers (Regulatory Innovation & Member Services) as an internal research prototype. It monitors the public meeting records of 63 state medical and osteopathic boards and is written for colleagues who follow licensing, discipline, and policy trends.

What

A searchable library of board agendas and minutes: the original PDFs, an AI summary of each meeting, a 12-month narrative per board, a cited national landscape report, and full-text search. Fifteen recurring topics are tagged — licensing, telehealth, disciplinary process, AI, and more.

Where

Sources are the boards' own public websites; every document in the system is a public record. The database and dashboard run on a single local workstation — this is not a public website or a hosted service.

When

Refreshed automatically on the first of each month, with on-demand refreshes anytime. Summaries cover a rolling 12-month window, and every page shows when its data was last collected.

How it works · guardrails · by the numbers

HowFrom board website to dashboard in five steps

1

Discover

Find each board's meeting-minutes page on its official website.

2

Collect

Download the original agendas and minutes; every file is checked before it goes in the library.

3

Extract

Pull the text from each PDF; scanned paper documents are transcribed page by page.

4

Summarize

AI writes a summary per meeting and per board under strict rules: only what the document says, with every claim cited.

5

Verify

An automated audit checks dates, names, and citations before anything reaches the dashboard.

UsesFour things it does well

  • Search everything at once. One search box covers the full text of every collected document — a term, a drug name, a statute.
  • Read a board's year in minutes. Each board has a 500-word narrative of its last 12 months, linked into each meeting.
  • Follow a topic nationally. Open a topic like AI or telehealth and see which boards discussed it and what each one said.
  • Start from the synthesis. The national landscape report reads across 57 boards, with citations back to the source minutes.

GuardrailsHow the AI is kept honest

  • Summaries may use only what appears in the source document — nothing is added from outside knowledge.
  • Every dated claim links to the source PDF, which stays one click away as the authoritative record.
  • A built-in check rejects any summary of a meeting that has no underlying document text before it reaches the dashboard.
  • After every refresh, a separate audit checks for date mismatches, cross-board mix-ups, and broken citations.
  • Meetings with no readable text show no summary at all — an honest blank, not a guess.

What it is

  • A research aid for reading public board records faster
  • A descriptive record of what boards discussed, in their own documents' words
  • An internal prototype — a working example of what this approach can do

What it is not

  • Not an official record — the linked source PDF is always the record
  • Not an assessment, ranking, or evaluation of any board's practices
  • Not a public product or a hosted service
